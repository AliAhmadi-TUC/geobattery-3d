Can a flooded mine store summer heat for winter?

Solar and wind flood the grid with cheap power in summer — exactly when nobody needs heat. A geobattery banks that surplus as warmth: pump hot water into the rock around a flooded mine drift while it's cheap, and draw it back when it's cold. This is a full-3-D thermal simulation of one real drift in the Reiche Zeche mine, asking a simple question — when does it actually work?

Single drift 10-m drift array

A lone drift sits in cold rock that never stops draining it — after a full decade it still returns only ~13%, as low-grade ~13 °C water. Pack drifts into a 10-m array and their warm halos overlap: the rock between them saturates and stops being a sink, so retention climbs past 98% by year 10. (In a closed array cell near-100% retention is really the geometry asserting itself — what counts is the grade it delivers: ~58 °C stored, ~25 °C at the tap.)

A year in the life of the drift

Outlet water temperature through one annual cycle

Charge → store → discharge → recover (surveyed drift, year 1).

Sixty-one days of charging drive the water to ~68 °C. Through storage and discharge that heat bleeds into the surrounding rock, and by the depths of winter the drift hands back water only a few degrees above ambient — a monthly buffer, not a seasonal store.

When it works

The study's real contribution is a dimensionless screening rule: whether an opening can hold heat across the seasons is fixed by its specific surface area A/V and the rock-to-water conductivity ratio — not by how hot you make it. This drift's A/V ≈ 1.9 m⁻¹ is far above the ≈ 0.6 m⁻¹ needed to return half your heat.

The fix is geometry, not temperature: a wider chamber, or an array of drifts close enough to share their thermal halos. You can tell which openings will work before you ever run the simulation.
