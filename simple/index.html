Can a flooded mine hold summer's heat until winter?

Solar power runs to surplus in summer — exactly when almost nothing needs heating — while the demand it could serve arrives in winter. Only seasonal storage bridges that gap. We test one real drift in the Reiche Zeche mine as a geobattery, with full-3-D conjugate heat-transfer, and derive a rule for when a mine can store seasonal heat at all.

2 One field, four materials

A single continuous temperature field spans water, rock, concrete and air, so heat crosses every interface by construction. A frozen Stokes flow carries the water; the solid answers with a one-way thermoelastic stress. Solved full-3-D in FEniCSx, and validated three ways.

Validated to 0.19 K on the store-end water temperature across three independent solvers — full-3-D FEM, a 2-D FEM disk, and a 1-D radial finite-volume model.

3 Why a single drift fails

The rock conducts heat away faster than the water holds it. In the four-month store, the thermal front reaches 3.87 m into the rock — far past the drift's own 1.05 m reach. What comes back is a sub-25 °C halo: a monthly buffer, not a seasonal store.

4 Read your mine before you dig

Here's the payoff. Retention collapses onto a few dimensionless groups — a Fourier number (Fo = αrock·tstore/Lc²) plus the rock/water conductivity and capacity ratios. Absolute size cancels, so any mine can read its own recovery off one master curve, from survey data alone. The only geometry that matters is the opening's reach Lc = 2·V/A.

The as-built drift (Lc = 1.05 m, A/V = 1.91 m⁻¹) sits a factor 3.3× too deep in the loss regime. To return half its heat it would need to widen to Lc ≥ 3.4 m — a chamber, or an array of drifts that share their thermal halos. (First-cycle recovery in gneiss, κ = 5, 123-day store.)

5 When it works: pack them together

Give the drift neighbours. In a 10-m array, each drift's warm halo overlaps its neighbour's, the rock between them saturates and stops being a sink — so almost nothing leaks away.

The answer

Not one drift — an array, or a chamber.

A lone drift returns only 7.3%. Remove the far-field sink and the same opening keeps nearly everything: the 3-D array cell climbs 27 → 98.5% over ten years, saturating after about six. Grade still lags quantity — 58 °C stored, 25 °C delivered.

6 Quantity vs grade

Geometry sets how much heat returns; the in-situ rock temperature only lifts how usable it is. Widen the opening and quantity climbs; warm the rock (T₀ = 30 °C) and every temperature rises — but part of what you "recover" is then native geothermal heat, not stored solar.

7 Under the hood

Full-3-D conjugate heat transfer in FEniCSx: a Taylor–Hood Stokes flow (frozen after solve), a consistent-flux recovery at the Dirichlet inlet, and a one-way thermoelastic solid that picks up ~68 K of wall heating and ~35 MPa of induced von Mises stress — about five times the in-situ load. The saddle-point Stokes system is symmetric-indefinite, so it's solved with a memory-bound direct factorization (MUMPS).

Geometry: 230.9 m³ of water, 441.5 m² wetted area, Lc = 1.05 m · gneiss krock/kwater = 5 · 10 → 80 °C duty cycle over charge / store / discharge / recover.
