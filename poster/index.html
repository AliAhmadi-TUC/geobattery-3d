Scale, before anyone over-reads this. One as-built drift absorbs 88.4 MWh per charge — about 60 kW averaged over the 61-day charge phase. The whole zone's 2023 surplus at f = 1.0 (50.2 GWh) is 569 drift-charges; a single peak surplus hour would fill 26 of them.

04 · when it does workRead your own mine off the plane

Retention collapses onto three dimensionless groups, so absolute size cancels: a mine reads its own first-cycle retention from survey data, before any mesh exists. Only two numbers enter — the specific surface a = Awetted/Vwater and how long you intend to seal it. That makes the criterion a plane, not a curve, and every iso-retention level on it is exactly a straight line, because Fo = α t a²/4.

04b · gradeThe pipe and the bill

The discharge as a pipe leaving the mine: across is the heat delivered so far, up is the temperature it comes out at — so the pipe's length is quantity and its height is grade, in one object. Beside it runs the free pipe: the undisturbed rock, which was always there and cost nothing. The compressor bill is the area between them, clipped at the building's header — green where your pipe runs warmer than the ground, red where it runs colder.

Curves are measured — run_decade_<scenario>/yNN_p2_discharge/metrics.csv, 186 steps. The x-axis is the net heat delivered, outflow minus the enthalpy the 10 °C injection carries back in; its endpoints equal recovered_kWh in the decade files exactly, so this panel and §05 agree. The heat pump is the model screening.json uses — half-Carnot, COP = 0.5·Tsink/(Tsink−Tsource) in kelvin — integrated step by step along the curve, which is exactly why the area is the bill: 1 px² is 27.17 kWh·K. The comparison is an idealised ground-source heat pump on the undisturbed rock with no approach temperature, which flatters the do-nothing case by a few kelvin; the sign of the 30 °C result survives that comfortably. Only these five runs exist — there is no continuous sweep over geometry, so unlike §04a this panel cannot follow your puck. Pumping and drilling energy are counted on neither side.

05 · ten years, five scenariosThe store and the ledger

Ten years of rock, one stratum per cycle. A stratum's height is the heat you injected that year and its width is the share that came back — so area is energy, 44 px² per MWh, the same in every scenario. Heat you stored and got back grows rightward out of the drift wall. Native ground heat, mined by the 10 °C discharge, grows leftward out of the rock, because it never came out of your injection.

tap a scenario on the shelf tap a stratum to light the decade up to that cycle tap the gold hatching to strip the ground heat

Five full-3-D decades, forty phases each. The vessel is the bulk water at end of storage above the undisturbed rock; the dashed rule across it is the tap — the mean outlet actually delivered over the 61-day discharge, which §04b integrates into what it is worth. In both 30 °C runs the tap sits inside the ambient block: the store hands back water colder than the ground it was cut into. The geothermal split is exact by linearity — with the discharge fixed at 10 °C, recovered(30 °C) = (50/70)·recovered(10 °C) + geothermal — but its published residual drifts from 1.6% in year 1 to 11% by year 10, so read the gold to a few percent, not to the decimal. The array cell is a closed unit cell with adiabatic symmetry planes, so its balance is pushed toward 100% by construction; its thinning strata are the honest counterweight, and the dashed rule is what a plant owner would actually have banked.

07 · numerical challengesThree theorems every Galerkin code lives with

Fix the value of a field on a boundary and you delete that node's equation. The flux through that boundary is not the slope of the computed answer — slopes converge slowly and are never exact. It is the reaction on the constrained degrees of freedom — the consistent boundary flux: the leftover of the very equation you deleted, exact on every mesh.

Drag to rotate — the loop keeps playing. Arrow lengths and the tilt of the balance are real output of a P1 solve running in your browser at every mesh shown.

Q = Σi∈Γ[ a(uh,φi) − ℓ(φi) ] — exact at every h · −k ∂uh/∂n|Γ = O(h) — never exact

Where this project meets it · pinning the inlet at 80 °C and summing k ∂T/∂n across it left the energy budget visibly open. Metered instead as the reaction — the consistent boundary flux of Douglas, Dupont & Wheeler and Carey, in the form of Gresho et al. — the balance closes to ~10−6 kWh per phase against 88.4 MWh stored: one part in 1011. Every number in FIG 5 is metered this way.

A sealed face is natural: write nothing, and the mathematics seals it. Pin two adjacent sealed faces to values instead and the grid nodes on their shared corner belong to both patches — two strong conditions arrive at one unknown, the system is over-determined, and the corner answers with spurious flux. The cure is to impose the seal weakly, by penalty: every node keeps exactly one equation.

x ∈ Γ₁∩Γ₂ : u = g₁ and u = g₂ — over-determined · the cure: add (γ/h)∮(u−g)v — weakly

Where this project meets it · the rock–water and the concrete–water interfaces meet at a corner ring of shared grid nodes. Sealing both with Dirichlet values over-constrained exactly that ring, so the no-flux condition is applied weakly, by penalty — the corner keeps one equation per node and passes nothing.

Minimise an energy and the landscape is a bowl: every direction uphill, and the cheap solvers — Cholesky, conjugate gradients — exist because of that shape. Add a constraint through a Lagrange multiplier and the solution becomes a saddle point: still symmetric, no longer definite, one negative direction per constraint.

Drag to rotate — the saddle is a 3-D fact, and the orbit is part of the argument.

[ A Bᵀ ; B 0 ][ u ; p ] = [ f ; g ] — symmetric but indefinite · no Cholesky, no CG

Where this project meets it · the water in the drift is incompressible and its pressure is a live Lagrange multiplier, so every time step factorises a saddle system — 17 110 negative pivots, about twice the memory of the equivalent definite solve; that ceiling, not the physics, is what capped the mesh.
